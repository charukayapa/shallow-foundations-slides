What is a Foundation?

Shallow (Spread)

A foundation that transmits the load of the building safely to the ground at a level close to the lowest floor of the building.

  • Strip Footings & Pads
  • Raft Foundations

Deep (Piled)

A foundation that transmits loads to deeper, more competent soil layers, far below the lowest floor.

  • Piled Foundations

NZ Code & Foundation Durability

Key Requirements

Foundations must be designed to last the life of the building.

  • NZ Building Code B1/VM4: Provides specific guidance on foundation requirements.
  • Durability: Materials must resist environmental factors (like moisture).
  • Rust Protection: Steel reinforcing (rebar) must have sufficient concrete cover to protect it from oxygen and water.

Understanding Building Loads

Dead Loads

Permanent, static loads that do not change.

  • The building's own mass (walls, floors)
  • Fixed equipment
  • Pressure from surrounding soil & water

Live Loads

Variable, non-permanent loads that can change.

  • Occupiers, furniture, and equipment
  • Wind forces
  • Earthquake forces

Wind Loads: Pressure vs. Suction

Wind is Not Just a Push

While wind creates pressure (pushing) on a building, it also creates suction (pulling) on the leeward (downwind) side and especially on the roof.

Foundations must be designed to anchor the superstructure, preventing uplift from these suction forces.

Load Types: UDL vs. PL

How Loads are Applied

  • Uniformly Distributed Load (UDL): The load is spread evenly over its support. A load-bearing wall creates a UDL on the footing below it.
  • Point Load (PL): The load is concentrated on a single point. A column creates a PL on the pad foundation below it.

Ground Settlement

Types of Settlement

Settlement is the gradual downward movement of the foundation as the soil consolidates (compacts) under the building's load.

  • Uniform Settlement: The entire building sinks evenly. (No cracks)
  • Tipping Settlement: The building tilts. (Often no cracks)
  • Differential Settlement: One part of the building sinks more than another. This is dangerous and causes cracks.

Foundation Type 1: Strip Footing

Continuous Strip Foundations

Used to support Uniformly Distributed Loads (UDLs), such as continuous load-bearing walls.

  • Traditional: Standard width and depth.
  • Wide Strip: Spreads the load over a larger area, used on weaker soils.
  • Deep Strip: Dug deeper to bypass weak soil and rest on a stronger stratum.

Foundation Type 2: Pad Foundation

Isolated Pad Foundations

Used to support Point Loads (PLs), such as individual or multiple columns. They are typically square or rectangular.

Pad and Beam: A system where isolated pads are connected by ground beams. This "locks" the pads together, increasing resistance to seismic loads.

Special Foundation Configurations

Continuous Column

Supports a full line of columns when they are too close for isolated pads.

Balanced Foundation

Connects pads to balance an offset (eccentric) load, preventing tipping.

Cantilever Foundation

Used to avoid obstructions (like boundaries or sewers) by cantilevering the load.

Foundation Type 3: Raft Foundation

Solid Slab Raft

A solid slab of concrete, reinforced in both directions. Best for lightweight structures.

Beam and Slab Raft

An inverted floor (beams + slab) used for increased loads and greater rigidity.

Cellular Raft

Top and bottom slabs with internal beams. Used for very high stresses and maximum rigidity.
